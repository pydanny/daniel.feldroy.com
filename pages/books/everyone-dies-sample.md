# Chapter 1 \- Aaron

Aaron grinned.

He had gotten this far, beating out larger men. His wiry strength was the result of a youth spent training to use the most powerful bows. He squatted down, wrapped his arms around the small boulder, and pulled it into his bare chest. He grunted and pushed his legs straight while carefully arching his back just a tad. He took a step forward, lifting the rock over the bar, and let go. He took a step back, lifted his arms, and drank in the roar of the crowd.

“Move out of the way,” growled the next contestant. He was a giant, bald, blocky figure of a man with  a powerful body. His craggy face had a bent nose from too many hits, and his massive hands had fingers that hadn't healed correctly. Aaron knew him as Bram, a professional competitor in wrestling, staff fighting, and throwing. His technique for lifting the rock mirrored that of Aaron’s, as did his success.

While the crowd cheered another man stepped forward. Aaron didn’t know him but recognized the strength in his workman’s frame. His face was ruddy and simple. He was also without a shirt, and his patched trousers were held up with a piece of old rope. He too made short work of the lifting rock, hefting it with explosive power.

“We have a three-way tie!” shouted the judge when the crowd quieted down. “But we only have one prize. These three strong men will go more rounds until two of them can’t lift the boulder over the bar.”

Aaron grinned once more at the crowd. Inside he felt some doubt about winning this event. Archery and murder were his trade, not lifting bodies or things the way the other two men clearly did. Besides, he had already won the archery contest, and had scored of a prize of coin for himself. The day wouldn’t be a total waste. 

“You’re up,” said the judge, pointing a finger at Aaron. The archer thought of giving up, but then saw a pretty woman looking at him with eager eyes. He knew he was handsome and her attention might lead to something later. So he nodded and stepped forward. 

Pushing the thought of winning or losing out of his head, Aaron squatted down to the lifting rock once more.

# Chapter 2 \- Melody

As Melody walked around the fair, through a crowd of onlookers she saw big men take turns lifting rocks. She recognized Aaron, another mercenary like herself looking for work. He was a fine looking man but she hoped he lost. In the war against the Southrons the archer had led plundering parties noted for their cruelty. Looking at his opposition she wished she had a coin to spare to bet against the bastard.

Her own contest, longsword play, was coming up soon, so she kept walking. A brisk walk followed by lunges was her preferred method for warming up.

Melody walked past vendors who called for her to buy their wares and she marked a couple food stalls for later. If she did well enough to gain employment with the King she would sample their goods. If she didn’t do well then she had a half loaf of coarse bread and a piece of cheese left over from breakfast. Confident she would do well, Melody also knew that prudence would keep her belly filled longer.

As she walked through the fairground people moved out of her way. Most of them were peasants and Melody was tall for a woman and wore metal armor. Over her shoulder were slung both of her longswords, the sharp one for fighting and the dull one for practice and competitions like this. The sharp one was peace bonded, tied into its scabbard.

Two people, a man and a woman in fine clothing of the sort nobles wore, didn’t get out of her way. Melody didn’t give it a second thought, stepping to the side to get past them. Then the man stepped and blocked her path. Melody stopped and studiously kept her eyes on the man’s overly ornate staff. He didn’t look like he wanted to fight, but Melody had learned that danger wasn’t always obvious. 

“Melody,” the man said. His voice was beautiful, high and clear. 

The swordswoman’s gaze flicked to his face. He had a youthful face in which were set eyes that were blank of emotion. That shocked her, she was used to being able to read people. “I don’t know you,” she said, taking a step back. “How do you know me?”

“I am blessed with knowledge,” the man said. “Count your strikes as you score them.”

“Count them?” Melody asked.

The man didn’t say anything in response and the silence between them stretched to the point of being uncomfortable.

“Shai is a man whose words carry unusual weight,” said the woman in a trained noble’s voice. Melody looked over and saw she was young, perhaps nineteen years of age. She was pretty with the sort of figure that Melody knew men liked. Around her neck was a necklace with a pendant in the shape of a stylized flame. Yet like the man it was the woman’s eyes that were unusual, for they were as old as time. “He is a seer and I’ve never known him to give bad guidance.”

“Will I win the longsword tournament?” Melody asked, anger rising. She had encountered charlatans of many kinds.

When another moment passed and Shai remained silent, the woman with him said, “I doubt he will say anything else on the matter. Nevertheless, it is good to heed the words of a seer if you want the best outcome. Fate is like that.”

At the mention of fate, Melody felt a surge of anger. “What’s next? A luck potion for as much coin as I carry?” she snarled. “I make my own luck and don’t believe in fate. Take your nonsense somewhere else.”

“Careful what you say to the king’s soothsayer,” the woman responded. “You might not believe in fate but fate doesn’t care what you believe in.”

Melody stomped past them. She marked them as a distraction, and tried pushing them out of her mind, attempting to focus on the matches ahead.

Yet the idea of using a loud count in the match wouldn’t leave her thoughts.

# Chapter 3 \- Trent

As the two armored fighters squared off, Trent sat on the log stump to judge them. For a month now his leg hurt when he stood too long without moving. He envied the competitors in front of him for the youthful movements; they did not appear to have his aches and pains.

The woman fighter, Melody, was in this match. He was interested to see how well she did. Longsword fighting required skill, speed, and strength. She might be strong for a woman but there would be stronger men here. She could make up for that with technique and speed.

Trent lowered his baton and the match began. The first clash ended in a blink of an eye. Melody had darted forward in an extremely deep lunge that required both flexibility and strength of leg. It was so quick her opponent didn’t have the chance to move, much less parry. The blunted point of her competition sword punched into her opponent’s torso with enough force to bend the blade and push him back, a clear and intimidating strike. Trent nodded at her impressive display of athleticism and started to call out the first hit.

“One!” Melody shouted as she turned away to return to her starting position, the springiness of the steel of her weapon bringing it back into its normal straight form. Trent was taken aback at the boldness of her statement and forgot to call out the hit. 

The other competitor nodded his readiness, keeping his longsword between himself and Melody. Then he flung himself forward when Trent lowered his baton once more.

Melody had batted aside her opponent’s blade and countered. “Two!” Melody shouted as she scored on her opponent, a hit as loud as a temple bell. 

Her opponent backed up to his starting position. Trent could see by the way he moved that he was already defeated. The man had given up. Not just to Melody’s superior skill and speed, but also to the inevitability of Melody’s loud counts. Around the match people exclaimed at the decisiveness of Melody’s skill and bets were made.

At that moment Trent wished judges could bet on participants. He would have bet everything he owned that Melody would win the longsword tournament.

# Chapter 4 \- Timo

Timo the woodcutter marveled at the quality of the wood used to construct the table on which their food was served. Black walnut cut into wide planks and finished to bring out the rich, dark, chocolate hues. Even stains and knife cuts from years of being used to serve food couldn’t diminish the lovely craftsmanship of the table.

He took another bite of the meat pie on his plate. The crust was perfectly flaky, the meat fresh, and the sauce rich with the luxurious taste of wine. He washed it down with excellent beer served in a tin mug. He had never eaten so well.

He felt a light jab to his ribs. Bram the wrestler had just elbowed him. Timo ignored it, he had beaten the other big man at rock lifting. That was why Timo was here with the other fairground champions — the king had invited them to a meal in his great hall. He could afford to be forgiving of a man he had beaten today.

Bram elbowed Timo again. “You fool,” the wrestler whispered. “The king is making a toast. Raise your cup.”

As Timo raised his beer mug, he kept his eyes focused on the table. He made sure he didn’t meet the gaze of anyone else at the table. He was bonded to the land, and putting eyes on his betters was something his Mum had told him to never do.

The king said words. Timo understood a few of them, but not enough for them to make any sense. The king’s voice sounded trained, but it wasn’t as strong as a town crier’s. Timo tried not to wonder what this meant. Then everyone gave a shout and drained their cups. Timo followed along.

Another man spoke. His voice was stunning like that of a great singer. Timo wondered if he would sing or give a sermon. Timo understood more of what he said. He said something about the Liche Queen and Timo shivered at the mention of her foul majesty. Then he took comfort in that he was in the heart of the kingdom and sitting inside the king’s own castle.

The king spoke again for a minute. Then people started to say “aye” one at a time. One, two, three, and higher than Timo could count said it. The last one was Bram sitting next to Timo.

Bram elbowed Timo yet again. “Say ‘Aye’,” the wrestler whispered. “Say it loudly, you can’t say no to the king.”

Timo cleared his throat and said “Aye” louder.

“Well done, gentle woodcutter,” said the king. Everyone cheered, Timo had never felt prouder. The king had spoken to him!

“A gold piece for each of us and employment in the king’s service,” Bram said to Timo, clapping the woodcutter on his shoulder. “All we have to do is survive and live another day.”

Timo didn’t know what ‘survive’ meant, but he liked living. He was very good at it, having done it since the day he was born.

# Chapter 5 \- Bram

As they ascended the stairs of the mage’s tower, Bram realized he had never been so high in his life. The thought of it made his guts tighten up. He fought the fear, trying desperately to shake it off. He focused on putting one leg in front of the other. They finally reached the top and Bram hid his sigh of relief behind a large grunt.

They passed through a door and entered a large circular room. Dark purple banners lined the walls and strange markings and symbols lined the floor. Six braziers burned around the room, providing some protection against the early morning chill. 

“Ready yourselves,” said Shai the soothsayer in his magnificent voice. “Konstantina will open the door. On the other side is danger.”

Bram leaned his heavy ash staff against his shoulder and checked his knife bandolier. He looked up and for a moment gazed hungrily at the shapely form of the young-seeming female mage as she chanted. Even her robe couldn’t hide her figure. Not wanting to antagonize a wizard with the eyes of the ages, he turned away to focus on the others.

Timo the woodcutter gawked openly at Konstantina. The man was an addled fool, but strong beyond measure. In his massive hands he carried a wood cutting axe. Like Bram he was wearing borrowed armor, one of the king’s gambesons. This was padded cloth jacket that would give him some protection. Bram doubted the man understood the gravity of what was happening, that they were being sent on what was probably a fool’s errand to stop the Liche Queen. 

The king’s soothsayer, Shai, held high his staff carved with intricate designs. In a measured voice, he spoke incomprehensible words that Konstantina listened to carefully. The seer’s face was serenely calm, and seeing that instilled confidence in Bram. If anyone knew how this was going to turn out, it was this man.

Aaron caught Bram’s eye. They had met in the rock lifting competition, both losing out to Timo. The archer darted his eyes at the soothsayer. Bram nodded at the archer and the archer nodded back. Bram knew his type, a mercenary coming back from foreign wars. Absolutely lethal with that bow and from the rakish scar on his cheek probably no slouch in close combat either. If they were going to go on a king’s quest it was hard to find a better man to go with.

The old warrior, Trent, looked grim and armed with enough gear to fight a war by himself. Poleaxe, longsword, buckler, and a heavy armor of plate and mail. He exuded old man strength and resolve. He respected Trent but worried for him. Shai had said this would be a matter of discretion over valor. Bram had reached thirty years and knew he couldn’t match a twenty-year-old’s running speed. He could only imagine how the old man’s joints would feel if it came to running for their lives.

Finally he looked at the swordswoman, Melody. He had heard of her, an up-and-coming tournament fighter in swords who had made a name in the same war as Aaron. Her head came up to his shoulder — considering his own height, that made her tall even for a man. She wore her armor as if it was weightless. It was plate over mail over gambeson with a helmet that had a grid to protect her face. She hadn’t put it on yet, so he had a chance to once more look at her face. The swordswoman wasn’t as fair as the mage Konstantina. Her face was sharper, with angular cheekbones. 

The flames in the six braziers rushed forward into the center of the room, centering on Konstantina. She wove her hands in an intricate pattern and the fire formed a ten-foot-high vertical circle in front of her. In the center the flames parted and on the other side was another place. A dozen or so haggard, man-like creatures waving chopping swords and axes in the distance were turning toward them. The part in the flames widened to the point that the circle could be passed through by a full grown person.

“The portal is open, go!” Konstantina shouted.

Bram hefted his staff and rushed forward with the others.

# Chapter 6 \- Konstantina

The stony ground on the other side of the portal felt comfortable, cool to the touch. For a moment Konstantina luxuriated in the moment of rest. Opening portals was never easy, and this close to the Liche Queen the rules changed and her portal magic was harder.

“Time to get up,” Shai said. He stood next to her. A prone dead figure in ragged dark clothes lay nearby, skull caved in. “We need to get out of this ravine. It’s going to be filled with hobgoblins, manifested evil spirits like the one I just killed.”

Konstantina groaned and pushed herself to her hands and knees. Knowing she would pay for it later she opened herself to the strength of the corrupted earth, letting it flow into her to dispel fatigue. What was normally an addictive rush that prudent mages had to be careful of felt horrid as the rotted interior of a coffin.

Fighting to keep her gorge down, she stood up. She shivered as she fought off the corruption she had just absorbed in order to be functional.

“You aren’t getting away,” Aaron shouted with a laugh. Konstantina watched as the archer nocked and loosed arrows at two fleeing hobgoblins, his movements smooth and practiced. The creatures were racing for a twist in the dry ravine in which they all stood. The arrows found their mark, one dropping like a stone while the other staggered forward. Before the inhuman creature could take five more steps the archer finished him with a third shot.

“One, two, three,” Melody numbered her strikes as she slashed into her opponent. With deft movements she batted aside its heavy chopping sword to open it up for three cuts. After the third it collapsed bonelessly to the ground.

More quietly, the others finished off the band of hobgoblins they had run into when they came through the portal. Bodies of the enemy lay crumpled and mangled. In contrast, all of her own group remained standing.

Konstantina studied some of the fallen enemies. She saw that the faces of each were uniquely wrong, some with eyes too far apart, lopsided mouths, warty skin. Even their limbs were often of the wrong proportions. It was as if they were created by a force intentionally negligent of how things were meant to be.

“The enemy is aware of where we are,” Shai said loud enough for everyone to hear. “We’re in her domain and the Liche Queen will have sensed the portal. She also knows when her creations die.”

“Then we must move quickly,” said Trent, the old soldier. “We go in a minute’s time.”

While others quickly cleaned their weapons with rags torn from the fallen, Aaron the archer ran forward. He carried a bone and leather clip for pulling arrows out of bodies. He got to work on the grisly task of retrieving arrows from corpses and dying hobgoblins.

“Never killed a hobgoblin before,” Bram said as he pulled a throwing knife out of one of the fallen. “Until now it’s only been outlaws and the like. I didn't enjoy killing people when I had to do it, this fight feels different.”

“That’s because the Liche Queen’s creatures have no souls, even if they resemble men,” Shai said. “We aren’t feeling the price that is normally paid for ending a life.”

Konstantina started to hear the faint echo of what sounded like the gleeful cries of the hobgoblins when they first arrived. “They are coming,” she said. “Which way?”

“That way,” Shai said, pointing in the opposite direction that the fleeing hobgoblins had tried to take.

Konstantina nodded and forced herself into a light run. The others could catch up.

The ravine turned and twisted. Boulders and fragments of trees and bushes lay here and there, a sign that this path wouldn’t be safe in the rain. The ravine narrowed and the debris forced her to climb in a few places. She was grateful that magic hadn’t just kept her appearance youthful; it gave her the agility of the teenage girl she had been when she started her apprenticeship. She heard the others also making their way forward, their breath and clatter of gear marking their progress.

“Wait!” called Melody the swordswoman just after Konstantina exited a turning, particularly narrow part of the ravine that was mostly free of debris.

Panting from exertion, Konstantina stopped. The jibbering of the hobgoblins was louder in her ears.

“Trent isn’t with us,” Melody said. “He stopped and told me to go ahead.”

“This isn’t for old men,” said Aaron, breathing easily even after their exertion. “He was a fiend in that fight.”

“We cannot wait,” gasped Shai. “We must go.”

Konstantina nodded and for a second turned around to continue. Then, unbidden, memories of her and Trent’s youth came to mind. Of what could have been had she not gone to study under the archmage, of the marriage between them that hadn’t happened. She remembered officiating his marriage to his wife; treating his children and grandchildren like nephews and nieces, using her powers to help keep them healthy.

She couldn’t just leave Trent behind.

Konstantina ran back the way they had come, ignoring the cries of the others. She went through the narrow turn and there was Trent. He was sitting on a boulder, his back slumped against the ravine wall. His helmet was off, set next to him on the boulder.

“Good, bare skin means I can make it better,” she said as she closed with him. “I can dispel the fatigue and make your lame leg better for a time.”

“No,” Trent said as he raised his hand between them. “You pay a toll for magic, especially when it comes to helping others. Go and protect the others, they’ll need your power.”

“Let me do this,” Konstantina pleaded. “You’ll die if you stay here.”

“This is a very defensible spot and I’m in heavy armor,” Trent said. “I’ll be able to hold them off for a very long time.”

“You’ll die!” Konstantina said, fighting back tears.

“Then it’s been a good life. I got to love you, and then raised a family. I’m going to be a great grandfather soon,” Trent said. “Doing this means I’m able to protect all of you.”

Their eyes met and held each other captive. She could hear the cries of the Liche Queen’s minions getting louder by the second. Konstantina knew she had to go now, that the hobgoblins would target her with everything they had at hand.

Trent stood up and reached for his helmet. Before he could lift it to his head Konstantina rushed forward and pressed her lips to his. For a moment the decades melted away and they were just a boy and girl in love.

Then she felt his armor, the scratch of his beard, remembered his wife, his descendants, and their oaths to king, queen, and country.

Konstantina broke the kiss and turned away. Started back toward the others.

“Goodbye,” Trent said behind her, his voice as heavy as Konstantina’s heart.

Tears coursed down Konstantina’s cheeks as she ran.

# Chapter 7 \- Shai

Doomed. They were all doomed.

Shai couldn’t let the others know of his despair. That he knew how each of them would die, including himself. Because if he did that they wouldn’t just fail, their deaths would be so much worse. So he swallowed another sigh and kept up with the others.

Konstantina caught up with them, face puffy from tears clearly wiped away.

“He’s staying,” Melody said as a statement, not a question. The swordswoman put her hand on the sorceress’ shoulder. “He’s making a last stand.”

For a moment everyone was silent. Then Aaron broke the silence, “We’ve got to get moving. I’ve been on enough raids to know unless you have a hole to hide in, you can’t stay in place.”

“Raids or reaving?” Melody said, glaring at the archer.

“Depends on which side you are on,” Aaron said with humor, matching her anger with his friendly-looking grin. Through his visions Shai knew that grin was a charade. Aaron’s good looks and easy charm hid a terrible capacity for violence against the weak and defenseless. Fate had chosen him not just for his skill, but also to exact justice for sins he had committed.

None of this Shai could reveal. He had to keep them moving toward the fatal destiny that would ensure the least painful death for his own person. “Let’s go,” Shai said. “We must make the best of Trent’s sacrifice.”

They started back on the path through the ravine. Hours later it opened into a dry waterfall overlooking a valley. Even though it was near noon dark clouds cast the ground below into gloom worthy of the grave. Their target lay under them, a spire of yellowed bone jutting through the heart of what had once been a pleasant mountain village.

“That’s where we end the Liche Queen?” Bram asked, pointing with his staff.

Shai nodded, hoping that they wouldn’t notice him shiver at the sight of the place from which none of them would return. “The mountain air is cold here,” he said, preempting any suspicion they might have. “I can guide us past the hobgoblin patrols.”

They began to climb down toward the valley floor. Their handholds were withered vegetation that still held the rock and soil of the valley in place. Halfway down Shai felt a release in the threads of fate, tension relieved as the plans of destiny were fulfilled.

The feeling told him Trent was dead.

While the feeling felt natural and right, to Shai it meant he was one step closer to his own ending. Shai fought the rising despair. He hated the curse of knowing his own death, that he had to be an agent of his own demise. 

Doomed. They were all doomed.
